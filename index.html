Decent car, not so decent design. The Nissan Juke was designed by Koji Wakabayashi, Takashi Utsunomiya, and Marten Wallgren back in 2009, and first debuted at the 2010 Geneva Motor Show in the March.

As far as the design goes, it is a very ugly car looking at it from the front and the back, with its bug-like appearance at the front, and its very bulbous rearend. We have not yet test-driven the car as of now, but we have plans to one day get hold of one and take it out for a spin, to see if, against all odds, the car's mechanics can make up for its shortfalls in the design department.

A future classic. This was one of the highest-rated cars when it was released, with it gaining numerous pretigious awards such as the golf receiving both Top Gear's Best Family Car award and What Car's Best Small Hatchback award.

While the non-GTI models, such as the Golf E (pictured) look a bit more basic and plain from the outside (and the inside), it is a very nice car to drive. The Golf E with the 1.4 litre petrol engine, which is what we tested, is very refreshing to drive compared with much newer cars we've driven, such as the Mini Cooper (2019) 1.6L petrol and the VW Polo (2022) 1.0L petrol cars, since they have a completely different feel because of the much newer technologies they utilise vs. the 2002 Golf.

Although the 0-60mph time is substantially longer than the two other cars mentioned above, you are able to get up steep hills in third gear, vs. the polo where you have to go up steep hills in second if going at a similar speed.

An excellent electric car. Much better than a Tesla. The EQB is as luxurious as any other Mercedes-Benz, as you would expect; the car comes in quite a few varients, such as the EQB 250, EQB 300, and the EQB 350. We have tested the EQB 350, and we have to say, it drives as sweet as a nut (as James May would say).

It is ultra powerful, rendering it super useful for small central reservations on duel-carriageways, doing drag races against Tesla owners, driving to Germany to have a cheeky go on the Autobahn, or racing LNER Azuma trains (though we wouldn't recommend doing the latter three, as we haven't done those either).

As for the interior trim, you are treated to a lovely combination of extremely comfortable part-leather seats, leather dashboards and door trims, and a very powerful touchscreen and digital drivers' dash running MBUX (Mercedes' replacement for their awful COMMAND system), allowing you to use Android Auto with ease.

We also have to mention that there is no visible nor noticible scratchy plastic trims anywhere in the car, as you would expect from any Mercedes-Benz.

Luxury, at its utmost finest. For years, Range Rovers and Land Rovers in general have had the reputation for constantly breaking down, having a lot of electrical faults and having very expensive costs for fixing minor things. But this is not the case with the Range Rover Sport, or any Land Rover from the last 15 years, since Land Rover made huge strides in boosting the reliability of their cars.

The Range Rover Sport is very nice to drive, with a super smooth 8-speed automatic gearbox and air suspension; the ride quality in the Sport is really good, with the driver having a commanding view over other traffic on the road. Plus, with the air suspension, bumps in the road are softened out, making potholes feel much less jarring. And as for design, my goodness, it is a real stunner -- the design of both the front and back have become the well-known signature style of Land Rover, meaning anyone who sees your car instantly knows you drive a Range Rover.

Posted 27/03/2025, 16:34

This car is excellent -- it has a massive community of Disco 3 enthusiasts, it has a lovely, rugged, classic Land Rover design, and best of all, it drives really lovely. However, there is one small issue: they are not as reliable in 2025 as they would have been back in 2006 when they were still brand new;

The Disco 3 we tested was from 2006, however, it had some issues with the air suspension where it would always be lowered due to an electrical fault, its steering would constantly be slightly out of alignment, the parking brake sensor was dodgy, and the ABS would activate sometimes when going round corners, which was a bit strange.
However, it was very nice as a towing car with a caravan, and was great for lugging stuff around since it had such a massive boot -- great for those weekends where you have to pick up new Ikea furniture.

A nippy and smooth car to drive. The Polo from 2022 (the R-line Polo specifically) was a very nice car to drive in our testing -- although it only had a 1.0L petrol engine, it must have been turbocharged, since its 0-60mph was even quicker than the 2002 Golf's, even though the Golf has a 1.4L engine;

As for the design, it is not really anything too special, since it looks very similar to just about every other Volkswagen released in the last 5 years or so -- with its sharp design features, and very basic and annoying touchscreen infotainment system.
